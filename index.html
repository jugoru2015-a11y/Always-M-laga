Understanding Málaga

Understanding a place changes how we relate to it.

Málaga is often seen through the lens of holidays: sun, the sea, good weather, and an easy sense of enjoyment. But behind that image, there is a lived city, shaped by routines, traditions, and small everyday gestures that rarely appear online.

This website brings together fragments of that everyday Málaga: specific places, local rituals, and familiar traditions that matter to people who live here.

Looking at these details helps place Málaga in context. Like many touristic cities, it is constantly changing: tourism brings movement and opportunity, but it also reshapes spaces and slowly pushes everyday culture into the background. Paying attention to what is usually overlooked becomes a way of understanding the city more fully.

Always Málaga is a quieter way of looking, a collection that holds onto what is still here. It is meant to add depth to the image of Málaga, for those who live in it, and for those who want to relate to it with a different perspective.

The Biznaga: Symbol of this project

The biznaga is a traditional handmade flower from Málaga, made by placing fresh jasmine blossoms onto a dried fennel stem. Its sweet scent is closely linked to summer nights, street life, and local craft, making it one of the city’s most recognisable symbols.

Travel with respect

Travelling with respect starts with awareness: every place you visit is also someone's home.

  • Look beyond the surface: learn a little about local culture, history, and daily life.
  • Respect shared spaces: neighbourhoods, streets, and traditions are not "tourist scenery."
  • Slow down: choose calmer, less crowded experiences over checklists.
  • Support local life: small businesses and family-run places help keep identity alive.
  • Be mindful of your impact: tourism changes cities, acting with care makes a difference.

Respectful travel is not about doing less, but about seeing more, with attention, curiosity, and consideration for the people who live there.

About me

My name is Julia González, and I am a Tourism and Translation & Interpreting student at the University of Málaga. I have always enjoyed travelling and learning about other cultures, but above all, I feel deeply connected to my hometown: Málaga.

As a tourism student, I am increasingly concerned about how many cities are changing due to mass tourism and how this process can lead to the loss of local identity and everyday culture. I am especially interested in cultural heritage, authenticity, and the small details that give places their character.

Using Málaga as an example, this project aims to raise awareness of these changes and encourage a shift in how we approach travel. Rather than consuming places, it invites us to understand them and to travel in a more respectful, conscious way.

— Julia González, Málaga
